## Design Context

### Users
Twitter/X followers, recruiters, coworkers, and engineering peers. Primary use case is discovery — someone wants to know more about Eric's career, typically around hiring decisions. The site needs to make the case quickly and convincingly: "I'd hire this person in a heartbeat."

### Brand Personality
Professional but fun. Deep care about craft and nuance. Three words: **crafted, confident, approachable**.

Not cold or corporate. Not whimsical or casual. The sweet spot is someone who clearly sweats the details but doesn't take themselves too seriously — like a well-designed tool that feels good to use.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Theme**: Dark-first. Dark mode is the brand identity; light mode is secondary/inherited.
- **References**: Linear.app (precision, restraint, dark-mode craft, smooth interactions), Clerk.com (polished developer-facing product, confident typography, clean hierarchy).
- **Anti-references**: The old cyan-gradient hero version of the site. Generic portfolio templates. Overly playful/illustrated personal sites. Corporate enterprise aesthetics.
- **Current direction**: Warm serif (Lora) + clean sans (Satoshi) + amber accent on zinc neutrals. The layout and typography hierarchy are working well. Evolution should deepen the craft rather than change direction.

### Design Principles
1. **Craft is the message** — Every detail signals engineering quality. Spacing, typography, and interactions should feel considered, not generated. The site itself is a portfolio piece.
2. **Confident restraint** — Bold where it matters, quiet everywhere else. Like Linear: the confidence comes from what's *not* there as much as what is.
3. **Dark-mode native** — Design for dark first, adapt to light. Dark isn't just an inversion — it's the primary brand expression.
4. **Hire-in-a-heartbeat fast** — Visitors are evaluating. Respect their time. Clear hierarchy, scannable content, no friction between landing and understanding who Eric is.
5. **Fun without trying** — Personality comes through in subtle details (micro-interactions, clever copy, unexpected textures like the pixel dither) rather than overt playfulness.
